I'm a Prospective Astronomy Graduate Student!

My name is Fiona Tülay Powers Ӧzyurt and I am a National Science Foundation post-baccalaureate astronomy research fellow at Maria Mitchell Observatory in Nantucket, Massachusetts working with Dr. Regina Jorgenson and Dr. Marc Rafelski at Space Telescope Science Institute. I hope to complete a Ph.D in astrophysics. My current research is in extragalactic astronomy, but I have a wide array of research experience that spans space-related inquiry: exoplanetary science, planetary science, astronomical instrumentation, and experimental physics. In May 2022, I graduated from Wellesley College with degrees in Astrophysics and Islamic Studies. Below is an overview of some of the projects I have been involved in.

Recent Work

Extragalactic Astronomy

My fellowship research focuses on galaxy formation and evolution. More specifically, I am characterizing neutral hydrogen (HI) gas reservoirs called Damped-Lyman alpha systems (DLAs) at high-redshift to better constrain simulations of galaxy formation and evolution. I analyze spectral data of background quasars at the highest possible redshifts taken with the Keck Observatory Cosmic Web Imager (Keck/KCWI), Echellette Spectrograph and Imager (Keck/ESI) and the Very Large Telescope (VLT) XSHOOTER spectrograph. In my first project, I analyzed IFU spectroscopic data to study the size, impact parameters, and star-formation rates of HI-selected galaxy targets. For my current project, I am using single-slit spectroscopic data to perform absorption-line analysis of DLAs to estimate the cosmic HI mass density and cosmic metal enrichment. I will present the results of this research in a poster entitled: “Evolution of the hydrogen incidence rate and mass density of damped Lyman-alpha systems out to z~5” at the 241st American Astronomical Society Meeting in January 2023.

Instrumentation/Laboratory

I have developed experimental physics laboratory and instrumentation projects of my own. In Fall 2021, I completed the course Modern Materials (PHYS 331), where I developed an experimental investigation into the best materials for a radio telescope. I synthesized thin film samples of silver, aluminum, and copper, analyzed their conductivity and infrared reflectance, and wrote a research article “Evaluating Reflectivity of Materials for Usage in Radio Telescopes.” In Spring 2022, I built and tested a Michelson interferometer and developed a method for analyzing the hyperfine structure of Rubidium as part of the course Experimental Laboratory Physics (PHYS 310): “Optimizing Frequency Calibration using Michelson Interferometry.” In August 2022, I participated in the astronomical instrumentation ISEE AstroTech Summer School at the University of California Berkeley, where I designed, built, and tested an optical wavelength astronomical spectrograph. As the team’s software specialist, I developed a data reduction pipeline in Python to reduce, process, and analyze our spectra.

Planetary Science

In September 2020, I joined the Perron Group at the MIT Department of EAPS and the COMPASSE (Cornell Comparative Planetology & Solar System Exploration) Research Group at Cornell University, studying Comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko. Broadly, my project served to place constraints on the comet’s dust-to-ice ratio. I analyzed image data taken with the Rosetta OSIRIS Narrow Angle Camera to model vertical erosion and deposition on the surface of 67P. I implemented Python, MATLAB, USGS ISIS (Integrated Software for Imagers and Spectrometers), and ArcGIS to create photoclinometric Digital Terrain Models (DTMs). Applying this method to hundreds of observed regions across the comet, I incorporated my measurements into a global sediment budget for 67P.
In summer 2022, I expanded my study of the surface morphology of 67P to include surfaces of cometary analogs. I compared the resolution-limited features on 67P that I had detected in previous research to those identified on other cometary analogs. My work demonstrated that constraints with pre-existing comet missions’ small temporal datasets and poor instrument resolution obscure their diverse morphologies and make surface features undetectable.

Exoplanets

In June 2019, I received a NASA Space Grant to work in an undergraduate cohort on a project to commission a research grade telescope with the goal of collecting exoplanetary data to do follow up photometry on candidate transiting exoplanets for NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS). As a member of the TESS Follow-up Observing Program, I conducted seeing-limited observations to look for signs of false-positive exoplanetary candidates, as well as to help refine ephemerides and light curves.
In 2021, I was awarded the Wellesley College Annie Jump Cannon Fellowship which supported my work selecting follow-up observing targets and creating reports for official submission to the TESS database. In February 2022, my TESS observations culminated in an Astrophysical Journal Letters publication on the discovery of a young, warm saturn-sized planet (TOI-1268b).
